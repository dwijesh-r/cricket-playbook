## The Headline

**The Most Dangerous Batting Lineup in the IPL Has a Bowling Problem It Cannot Ignore.**

SRH's 2026 squad reads like a fantasy draft: Head, Abhishek, Klaasen, Livingstone, Cummins. Five players with strike rates above 150 since 2023. A powerplay batting SR of 177.6 in 2024 that rewrote the record books. Yet the franchise has missed the playoffs in two of the last three seasons because a bowling attack that leaks 9.09 economy in the middle overs and 11.13 at the death since 2023 keeps giving back what the batting earns. The question for 2026 is not whether SRH can score. It is whether they can stop anyone else from doing the same.

---

## The Story

The 2024 season was supposed to be the beginning. Travis Head and Abhishek Sharma formed arguably the most destructive opening partnership in IPL history, combining for 1,051 runs at a powerplay SR of 177.6. Heinrich Klaasen scored 479 runs at 163.5, anchoring the middle overs with the authority of a player who understands that finishers finish. SRH made the final for the first time since 2018, losing to Kolkata Knight Riders but announcing themselves as a franchise built on unapologetic aggression.

Then came 2025, and the regression. Six wins in 14 matches. A 42.9% win rate (46.2% in decided matches) that kept them out of the playoffs. Head's SR dropped from 184.7 to 150.8, a 33.9-point decline. Nitish Kumar Reddy's SR fell from 137.1 to 113.0. The bowling, which had been papering over cracks all along, could not compensate: 10.30 economy in the powerplay, 9.13 in the middle, 11.14 at the death. The total of 194 average score in 2025 was the highest in the franchise's three-season window, but the bowling conceded at rates that made even 190+ totals feel inadequate.

The off-season response was surgical rather than radical. SRH retained their core: Klaasen (23.00 Cr), Cummins (18.00 Cr), Head (14.00 Cr), Abhishek (14.00 Cr). The marquee addition was Liam Livingstone (13.00 Cr, auction), a right-handed all-rounder whose 234.0 death SR across 100 balls (MEDIUM) and dual-spin capability (off-spin to left-handers, leg-spin to right-handers) fills the one gap SRH's lineup lacked: a genuine middle-order enforcer who can also bowl four overs. The acquisition of Ishan Kishan (11.25 Cr, retained from 2025) provided a left-handed wicketkeeper-batter to complement Klaasen's right-handed power.

The central question for 2026 is balance. SRH's batting lineup is, on paper, the most explosive in the tournament. But the bowling attack relies on Pat Cummins (9.32 economy, 110.7 overs, HIGH) and Harshal Patel (9.91 economy, 140 overs, HIGH) to carry a unit that has no frontline spinner of genuine quality. Zeeshan Ansari's 9.47 middle-overs economy across 30 overs (MEDIUM) is the best spin option, and that number is 0.71 runs above the league middle-overs average. If the bowling can tighten even marginally, the batting has enough firepower to outscore anyone. If it cannot, SRH will continue to be the team that entertains everyone and frustrates its own fans.

## Off-Season Changes

**Captaincy:** Pat Cummins (18.00 Cr, retained) continues as captain. An Australian Test captain leading an IPL franchise with the most aggressive batting philosophy in the tournament is an interesting tactical tension. Cummins' own IPL economy of 9.32 (110.7 overs, HIGH) is adequate, not elite. His captaincy will be judged on whether he can extract discipline from a bowling unit that has historically traded control for wickets.

**Key Departures:** Bhuvneshwar Kumar (released), T Natarajan (released), Marco Jansen (released), Aiden Markram (released), Rahul Tripathi (released). The pace bowling losses are significant. Bhuvneshwar was the franchise's all-time wicket-taker and provided sub-8.0 new-ball economy. Natarajan's 19 wickets in 2024 at 8.48 economy gave SRH a left-arm death option they no longer possess. Jansen's left-arm pace provided variety. Losing all three pace bowlers from the 2024 final squad and replacing them with Shivam Mavi (0.30 Cr) and various uncapped options is a gamble on existing depth.

**Key Retentions:** SRH retained 16 players totalling 112.30 Cr. The retention strategy was clear: keep the batting core intact at any price. Klaasen at 23.00 Cr, Cummins at 18.00 Cr, Head and Abhishek at 14.00 Cr each represent a franchise that believes its identity is defined by its batting.

**Auction Strategy:** Total squad spend of 115.20 Cr across 25 players. Livingstone (13.00 Cr) was the only significant auction buy. The remaining 8 auction purchases totalled 2.70 Cr, all at base price (0.30 Cr each). This was a "keep the band together" approach with one strategic addition, not a rebuild.

**What's Structurally Different from 2025:** Two changes matter. First, Livingstone's arrival gives SRH a genuine sixth bowling option who can also bat in the top 6, reducing the reliance on Nitish Kumar Reddy's expensive part-time medium pace (11.01 economy, 23.2 overs, MEDIUM). Second, the bowling unit has shifted from a Bhuvneshwar-Natarajan-Jansen pace trio to a Cummins-Harshal-Unadkat-Malinga configuration, which is older and less left-arm heavy. The net effect: more batting depth, less bowling variety.

## New Additions

### Liam Livingstone (13.00 Cr, Auction)

The headline buy and the missing piece. Livingstone strikes at 153.1 across 328 balls since 2023 (MEDIUM), but the phase breakdown tells the real story: 234.0 SR at the death (100 balls, MEDIUM) with a 34.4% boundary rate. That death-overs number is 63.8 points above the league average of 170.2. He bowls off-spin to left-handers and leg-spin to right-handers, giving SRH tactical flexibility they lacked. His 9.14 economy across 28 overs (MEDIUM) is adequate. The concern: his since-2023 powerplay sample is just 39 balls (LOW), producing an 84.6 SR — too small to draw firm conclusions but directionally suggesting he needs significant time to get going. His PP contribution at SRH will be an unknown quantity until a larger sample emerges. At Uppal, where pace dominates (Pace SR: 19.4, Spin SR: 26.5), his pace-hitting prowess (220.0 SR vs Fast, 70 balls, MEDIUM) is perfectly suited. His spin-hitting against left-arm orthodox (84.2 SR, 38 balls, LOW) is a genuine vulnerability that opposition captains will note.

### Jack Edwards (0.30 Cr, Auction)

An Australian all-rounder with zero IPL experience but a strong cross-tournament bowling profile: 8.5 economy across 882 T20 balls in BBL and MLC (trust score: 50). His batting (122 SR across 693 T20 balls, HIGH cross-tournament) is functional rather than explosive. He fills the bench role of an overseas pace all-rounder who can provide cover for Cummins if needed.

### Shivam Mavi (0.30 Cr, Auction)

A familiar IPL name at a base-price gamble. Mavi's IPL bowling career shows 8.86 economy across 70.2 overs (MEDIUM) with 21 wickets. His recent form (L10: 9.42 economy) shows a slight uptick, but at 0.30 Cr, the risk is minimal. He provides genuine pace depth behind the frontline four seamers.

### Other Auction Buys

Shivang Kumar (wrist-spin, 0.30 Cr), Krains Fuletra (wrist-spin, 0.30 Cr), Salil Arora (wicketkeeper, 0.30 Cr), Amit Kumar (leg-spin, 0.30 Cr), Onkar Tarmale (pace, 0.30 Cr), Sakib Hussain (pace, 0.30 Cr), and Praful Hinge (pace, 0.30 Cr) are all uncapped development picks at base price. ## Team Style Analysis

SRH's identity crystallized in 2024 and survived, bruised but intact, through 2025. This is a team built on one principle: outscore, don't outbowl.

**2023: Before the Revolution**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 125.2 | 9.30 |
| Middle | 134.5 | 8.50 |
| Death | 154.2 | 10.30 |

In 2023, SRH were a bottom-half franchise. Four wins. 28.6% win rate. A powerplay SR of 125.2 was 21.1 points below the league average. The batting was passive, the bowling was respectable, and the results were predictable. Nothing about this squad suggested what was coming.

**2024: The Year Everything Changed**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy | Delta from 2023 |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 177.6 | 9.48 | Bat: +52.4 / Bowl: +0.18 |
| Middle | 143.1 | 9.58 | Bat: +8.6 / Bowl: +1.08 |
| Death | 166.7 | 11.91 | Bat: +12.5 / Bowl: +1.61 |

A 52.4-point jump in powerplay batting SR. That is not a tactical adjustment. That is a philosophical transformation. Head (184.7 SR) and Abhishek (195.2 SR) simply chose violence from ball one. The cost was elsewhere: bowling economy deteriorated in every phase, with the death overs ballooning to 11.91. SRH reached the final because they scored enough to survive their own bowling. Nine wins. 56.3% win rate.

**2025: The Hangover**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy | Delta from 2024 |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 150.2 | 10.30 | Bat: -27.4 / Bowl: +0.82 |
| Middle | 155.4 | 9.13 | Bat: +12.3 / Bowl: -0.45 |
| Death | 173.8 | 11.14 | Bat: +7.1 / Bowl: -0.77 |

The powerplay regression was inevitable. From 177.6 to 150.2, a 27.4-point drop. Head's SR fell from 184.7 to 150.8. But the middle overs improved: 155.4 SR (up 12.3 from 2024), driven by Klaasen's continued dominance and Abhishek's ability to carry his aggression deeper into innings. Death batting hit a franchise-best 173.8. The bowling showed marginal improvement in the middle and death, but the powerplay economy of 10.30 was the worst in SRH's three-year window.

**Squad-Building Philosophy:** SRH are the IPL's purest expression of batting-first ideology. Seven of their top 8 are bat-first selections. The bowling is designed to take wickets through pace (Cummins, Harshal, Unadkat, Malinga) rather than contain through spin. At Uppal, a pace-friendly venue (Pace SR: 19.4 vs Spin SR: 26.5), this is at least directionally correct. But the absence of a quality frontline spinner means the middle overs are bowling's weak link, and the franchise is structurally limited in its ability to fix it without changing the overseas balance.

### Chase Results by Target Band

### What This Means for 2026

SRH's win rates are context-neutral (44.0% batting first, 44.4% chasing), but the phase data reveals a structural tension: the death-overs chasing SR collapses to 152.4 (19.0 points below setting), while the bowling is 0.64-0.78 runs more expensive when defending. SRH are better at bowling first but worse at batting second in the death -- two forces that cancel out at the win-rate level. The implication: context matters less than phase execution.

The coaching staff's priority: regardless of batting order, sustain middle-overs intent above 145 SR and ensure Klaasen arrives at the death with overs to spare. If batting first, the instruction must be to bat as if chasing 190 from ball one. The 2024 powerplay was built on that principle. The 2025 regression occurred because the intent dropped.

## Keys to Victory

**1. Reverse the Powerplay Regression**

SRH's powerplay batting SR dropped from 177.6 in 2024 to 150.2 in 2025 -- a 27.4-point collapse that was the single biggest driver of their season decline. Head's SR fell from 184.7 to 150.8, and the bowling economy worsened from 9.48 to 10.30 in the same phase. Restoring the 2024 intent from ball one is non-negotiable. Abhishek's L10 SR of 197.1 suggests the aggression is there individually. The coaching staff must ensure the opening partnership attacks from the first over regardless of whether they are setting or chasing, because SRH's win rates are identical across both contexts (44.0% vs 44.4%).

**2. Klaasen Must Face 250+ Balls at the Death Across the Tournament**

Klaasen's 206.5 death SR (246 balls, MEDIUM) is SRH's most irreplaceable asset. To maximize it, he needs to bat through the middle overs (161.5 SR at that phase) and arrive at the death with at least 10-15 balls already faced. If he enters at the death cold (after a middle-overs collapse), his PRESSURE_PROOF rating means he will maintain quality, but the volume of death-overs balls faced determines whether SRH score 190 or 210. The coaching staff should build the batting order around ensuring Klaasen bats overs 10-20 in every match.

**3. The Middle-Overs Bowling Must Find a Sub-9.0 Economy Option**

SRH's 9.09 middle-overs economy since 2023 is 0.33 above the league average. Among the frontline options: Cummins at 7.72 (36 overs, MEDIUM) is the best, but he bowls only 32.5% of his overs in the middle. Eshan Malinga's 7.69 (13 overs, LOW) is promising but unproven. Livingstone at 9.68 (25 overs, MEDIUM) is expensive. The coaching staff needs to commit to bowling Cummins and Malinga through overs 7-12 and using Livingstone's spin as a change-of-pace option rather than a primary middle-overs weapon. Without a sub-9.0 economy across 40+ middle overs from at least one bowler, the bowling will continue to leak the runs that the batting earns.

**4. The Overseas Balance Must Not Shift**

Head, Klaasen, Livingstone, and Cummins occupy all four overseas slots. Kamindu Mendis (0.75 Cr) and Brydon Carse (1.00 Cr) wait on the bench. If injury forces a change, the trade-off is painful: dropping Livingstone for Mendis removes 234.0 death SR and adds 135.3 SR with a 8.57 bowling economy. Dropping Cummins for Carse removes the captain and primary new-ball bowler. The fitness of all four overseas starters across 14 matches is a non-negotiable condition for SRH's campaign.

## Andy Flower's Scouting Report

**Tactical Identity:** Outscore, don't outbowl. SRH are built to post 200+ or chase any target through the sheer force of their batting order. The bowling exists to contain, not dominate.

**Structural Strengths:**
- Powerplay batting aggression: 152.3 SR since 2023, 6.0 points above league average (1,548 balls, HIGH)
