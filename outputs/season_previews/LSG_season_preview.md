## The Headline

**The Talent Is Obscene. The Structure Is the Question.**

Lucknow Super Giants enter 2026 with the most expensive player in IPL history (Rishabh Pant, 27.00 Cr), a middle-order destroyer in Nicholas Pooran (183.9 SR, 751 balls, HIGH), three genuine all-rounders, and a pace attack headlined by Mohammed Shami. On paper, the batting firepower is staggering. But LSG's 2025 season ended with 6 wins from 14 matches and a bowling economy that hemorrhaged 10.73 in the powerplay. The talent ceiling is top-two. The structural floor is eighth. Which version shows up depends on whether the bowling can hold what the batting promises to deliver.

---

## The Story

The trajectory is unmistakable: 8 wins in 2023, 7 in 2024, 6 in 2025. Three consecutive seasons of declining results for a franchise that reached the playoffs in its debut year (2022) and has been sliding since. The 2025 season was not a collapse in the way CSK experienced; it was a slow erosion. LSG's batting actually improved dramatically (158.7 middle-overs SR, up from 127.4 in 2023), but the bowling fell apart. A 10.73 powerplay economy and 11.27 death-overs economy in 2025 meant that no matter how many runs the batting unit scored, the bowling was returning the favour with interest.

The off-season response was a structural overhaul at the top. KL Rahul, the franchise's founding captain and defining presence, departed to Delhi Capitals (14.00 Cr, retained). In his place: Rishabh Pant (27.00 Cr, retained), the most expensive retention in IPL history, arrives as captain and middle-order anchor. Pant's 146.2 SR across 489 balls since 2023 (MEDIUM) and 210.0 death-overs SR (100 balls, MEDIUM) inject a dimension LSG never had under Rahul's cautious stewardship. The franchise also moved decisively in the auction, acquiring Wanindu Hasaranga (7.00 Cr), Josh Inglis (8.60 Cr), and Anrich Nortje (2.00 Cr) to address specific gaps in spin bowling and overseas depth.

The central question for 2026 is whether Pant can impose structure on a squad that has talent in abundance but coherence in doubt. LSG's batting features four players with career SRs above 145 (Pooran at 183.9, Abdul Samad at 158.0, Marsh at 158.1, Pant at 146.2), which should theoretically produce 190+ totals regularly. But the bowling unit's combined economy of 10.17 in 2025 was the second-worst in the league. Adding Hasaranga (9.07 economy, 70 overs, MEDIUM) and hoping Shami (9.25 economy, 95.2 overs, MEDIUM) returns to his powerplay-elite form after a form-disrupted stretch (11.42 L10 economy) are the two bets that will determine the season.

If the pace trio of Shami, Avesh Khan, and Mohsin Khan can compress the powerplay economy below 9.50, and if Hasaranga stabilises the middle overs alongside Digvesh Rathi, LSG have the batting firepower to reach the playoffs. If the bowling reverts to 2025 levels, the most expensive squad in the tournament will watch the knockout rounds from the commentary box.

## Off-Season Changes

**Captaincy:** Rishabh Pant (27.00 Cr, retained) takes over as captain, replacing the departed KL Rahul. Pant captained Delhi Capitals from 2021 to 2024 (22W-24L, 47.8% win rate), but his aggressive instincts align with LSG's batting-heavy roster. At 28, he inherits a squad built for attack and must now figure out how to defend.

**Key Departures:** KL Rahul (to DC, 14.00 Cr) is the headline exit. Rahul accumulated 2,265 runs for LSG across three seasons (2022-2024) at 136.1 SR with a conservative, top-of-innings approach that defined the franchise's identity. His departure creates both a batting void at the top and a philosophical shift: LSG under Rahul were measured and strategic; under Pant, they will be aggressive and instinctive. Quinton de Kock, Marcus Stoinis, and Krunal Pandya are also gone from the 2024 squad, representing significant losses in experience and all-round depth.

**Key Retentions:** Pooran (21.00 Cr), Mayank Yadav (11.00 Cr), Avesh Khan (9.75 Cr), Abdul Samad (4.20 Cr), Ayush Badoni (4.00 Cr), Mohsin Khan (4.00 Cr), Mitchell Marsh (3.40 Cr), Aiden Markram (2.00 Cr). The retention of Markram at 2.00 Cr is remarkable value for a South African international who strikes at 136.3 (670 balls, HIGH) and offers part-time off-spin.

**Auction Strategy:** LSG spent 119.35 Cr on 21 players. The marquee auction buys were Josh Inglis (8.60 Cr), Wanindu Hasaranga (7.00 Cr), Shahbaz Ahmed (2.40 Cr), and Anrich Nortje (2.00 Cr). The Shami trade (10.00 Cr) was the off-season's boldest move: a 35-year-old pace bowler whose powerplay economy of 8.67 (66 overs, MEDIUM) is among the best in the league, but whose last 10 matches showed 11.42 economy. LSG are betting on peak Shami, not recent Shami.

**What's Structurally Different from 2025:** Two fundamental shifts. First, the batting philosophy changes from Rahul's accumulation to Pant's aggression. Second, the bowling attack gains a genuine spin-bowling all-rounder in Hasaranga, who provides something LSG have lacked since inception: a world-class wrist-spinner who can bowl in all three phases. The squad is now built around four overseas pillars (Pooran, Marsh, Hasaranga, and one of Inglis/Nortje/Markram) rather than the previous three-plus-rotation model.

## New Additions

### Mohammed Shami (10.00 Cr, traded from Gujarat Titans)

34 wickets at 9.25 economy across 95.2 overs since 2023 (MEDIUM). Shami's powerplay numbers are the headline: 8.67 economy with 22 wickets in 66 powerplay overs (MEDIUM), a strike rate of 18.0, and a 49.0% dot ball rate that suffocates scoring. He replaces the structural hole LSG had at the top of the innings, where the 2025 powerplay economy of 10.73 was a full run and a half above the league average. The concern is recent form: his last 10 matches showed 11.42 economy, a 2.62-run spike from career. At 35, the line between elite and expired is thin. If peak Shami shows up, LSG's bowling picture transforms overnight.

### Josh Inglis (8.60 Cr, auction)

162.6 SR across 171 balls since 2023 (MEDIUM), with 278 runs in 11 innings. Inglis is a versatile wicketkeeper-batter who can bat anywhere from opener to middle order. His 166.2 powerplay SR (77 balls, LOW) suggests genuine aggression at the top, while his 160.5 middle-overs SR (76 balls, LOW) provides middle-order flexibility. At 8.60 Cr, he competes with Markram and Nortje for the fourth overseas slot. The sample size is frustratingly small for a player of this price, and the LOW confidence on his phase data means projections carry significant uncertainty.

### Wanindu Hasaranga (7.00 Cr, auction)

20 wickets at 9.07 economy across 70 overs since 2023 (MEDIUM). Hasaranga is the spin-bowling all-rounder LSG have been missing. His death-overs economy of 8.00 (7 overs, LOW) is intriguing but sample-limited. His middle-overs economy of 9.24 (58 overs, MEDIUM) is adequate, not elite, but his 4 powerplay wickets in just 5 overs (LOW) hint at a captain who could use him in unconventional phases. The batting contribution (uncapped in IPL batting data) is a bonus if it materialises rather than a number to bank on.

### Anrich Nortje (2.00 Cr, auction)

18 wickets at 10.94 economy across 69 overs since 2023 (MEDIUM). Nortje's value is raw pace and death-overs hostility, but his 12.70 death economy (30 overs, MEDIUM) is alarming. His recent form (12.57 economy in L10) suggests a bowler whose express pace is being punished more frequently. At 2.00 Cr, he is bench insurance rather than a first-choice XII member, but his ceiling (when fit and firing at 150 kph) remains the highest of any pace bowler in the squad.

### Shahbaz Ahmed (2.40 Cr, auction)

124.9 SR across 213 balls since 2023 (MEDIUM). A left-arm orthodox all-rounder who provides batting depth and a secondary spin option. His 149.1 death-overs SR (110 balls, MEDIUM) with the bat gives LSG a left-handed lower-order option. His bowling in the IPL is limited (no significant phase data), but his left-arm orthodox style fills a variety gap in a squad dominated by right-arm options.

### Arjun Tendulkar (0.30 Cr, traded)

Uncapped in IPL with meaningful data. A left-arm pace option who provides squad depth. Development pick at minimum price.

## Team Style Analysis

LSG's three-season evolution tells a story of a franchise that found its batting after losing its bowling.

**2023: The Conservative Start**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 124.1 | 9.15 |
| Middle | 127.4 | 7.59 |
| Death | 156.5 | 9.68 |

The inaugural full-strength season (2023) was defined by restraint. A 124.1 powerplay SR was among the lowest in the league, reflecting KL Rahul's anchor approach. The bowling was the strength: 7.59 middle-overs economy was elite, and the 9.68 death economy was competitive. This was a team that won through bowling discipline, not batting aggression.

**2024: The Middle Ground**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy | Delta from 2023 |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 131.0 | 9.84 | Bat: +6.9 / Bowl: +0.69 |
| Middle | 141.4 | 8.52 | Bat: +14.0 / Bowl: +0.93 |
| Death | 161.7 | 11.80 | Bat: +5.2 / Bowl: +2.12 |

The batting improved across every phase, particularly in the middle overs (+14.0 SR). But the bowling started to crack. Death-overs economy spiked from 9.68 to 11.80, a 2.12-run deterioration that signalled structural weakness. The death bowling was becoming a liability.

**2025: Batting Breakout, Bowling Breakdown**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy | Delta from 2024 |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 147.4 | 10.73 | Bat: +16.4 / Bowl: +0.89 |
| Middle | 158.7 | 9.52 | Bat: +17.3 / Bowl: +1.00 |
| Death | 166.2 | 11.27 | Bat: +4.5 / Bowl: -0.53 |

The batting transformation was complete. A 158.7 middle-overs SR is elite by any standard, driven largely by Pooran's 183.9 career SR and the introduction of aggressive middle-order options. But the bowling collapsed: 10.73 powerplay economy (1.47 runs above the league average of 9.26) and 9.52 middle-overs economy (0.76 above the league average of 8.76) made it impossible to defend any total consistently.

**Squad-Building Philosophy:** LSG have pivoted from a bowling-first franchise to a batting-first powerhouse in three seasons. The 2026 squad doubles down on this identity: Pant, Pooran, Marsh, Samad, Badoni, and Inglis represent six batters who can strike above 140 SR. The bowling responsibility falls heavily on Shami's powerplay mastery, Avesh's death-overs workhorse role, and whichever spinner (Hasaranga, Rathi, or both) can control the middle overs. The bet is clear: outscore the opposition. Whether the bowling can hold up long enough to make that strategy viable is the season's defining question.

### What This Means for 2026

The data is unambiguous. LSG's batting unit performs significantly better when setting (149.3 SR vs 138.4 chasing), and the bowling unit defends more effectively than it restricts when bowling first. The one exception is Pooran. His 194.9 chasing SR means that if LSG find themselves batting second, the strategy should be structured around keeping Pooran in the middle for as long as possible. Build the chase around his ability to accelerate, not around cautious accumulation in the powerplay.

If LSG can set 180+ totals (which their batting depth suggests is achievable), the defending bowling economics suggest a win rate significantly above their chasing record. The 2026 season should be built around a bat-first identity.
