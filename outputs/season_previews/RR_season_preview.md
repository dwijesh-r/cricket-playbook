## The Headline

**The Royals Rebuilt Their Spine in a Single Window. Now Comes the Hard Part: Making It Work.**

Rajasthan Royals won 9 of 15 matches in 2024, made Qualifier 2, and looked like a franchise on the rise. Then 2025 happened: 4 wins in 14 matches, a death bowling economy that ballooned to 12.37, and a finish that saw 9 losses in their last 10 games. The response was surgical. Ravindra Jadeja (17.50 Cr, traded from CSK), Sam Curran (10.00 Cr, traded from PBKS), and Ravi Bishnoi (7.20 Cr, auction) arrive to fill the structural gaps that dismantled the 2025 campaign. This is no longer the Samson-Buttler-Chahal franchise. It is, for the first time in modern IPL history, a Jaiswal-led, allrounder-heavy squad built to compete across all three phases. Whether these new pieces mesh is the only question that matters.

## The Story

The 2025 season was the unravelling that the underlying data had been warning about since 2024. Rajasthan Royals finished 9th with a 28.6% win rate, their worst return since 2020. The squad that reached Qualifier 2 in 2024 (60.0% win rate) lost Jos Buttler to retirement, Yuzvendra Chahal to a trade, and Trent Boult to a release. The replacements did not fill the gaps. Wanindu de Silva, Maheesh Theekshana, and Jofra Archer combined for 33 wickets but at a collective economy that bled runs at the death: 12.37 economy in overs 16-20, compared to 10.49 the year before. The bowling unit that had contained opponents to 8.77 in the middle overs in 2023 conceded 9.39 in 2025. That 0.62-run-per-over regression across 14 matches equated to roughly 33 runs per match handed to the opposition in phases where games are decided.

The batting, paradoxically, improved. Powerplay SR surged from 137.0 in 2024 to 174.4 in 2025, driven almost entirely by Yashasvi Jaiswal (18.00 Cr, retained), who struck at 177.4 in the powerplay across 208 balls (HIGH). Riyan Parag (14.00 Cr, retained, captain) averaged 160.4 SR, up from 140.8 in 2024. The middle order stabilized through Dhruv Jurel (14.00 Cr, retained), whose 152.1 SR was a 16.7-point improvement on 2024. The problem was not a lack of runs. It was that the bowling attack could not defend them.

The off-season response addressed the bowling deficit directly. Ravindra Jadeja provides middle-overs control at 7.79 economy across 135.2 overs (HIGH) and adds a left-arm orthodox option RR have lacked since Ashwin's departure. Sam Curran offers death-overs flexibility: 183.9 SR at the death with the bat (248 balls, MEDIUM) and 11.33 economy as a death bowler (29.8 overs, HIGH). Ravi Bishnoi at 8.57 middle-overs economy (106 overs, HIGH) gives the spin attack a second dimension alongside Jadeja. Combined with Jofra Archer's return to full fitness (12.50 Cr, retained), this is a squad built around the principle that if the bowling holds, the batting will take care of itself.

The central question for 2026 is integration. Jadeja has never played for RR. Curran is a PBKS product adapting to a new environment. Bishnoi returns to a franchise that last employed him in 2022. The individual pieces are proven. The collective chemistry is unproven. If these new additions find their rhythm by match 5, RR have the roster depth to challenge for a top-four finish. If the transition stutters, the 2025 spiral has shown how quickly this franchise can fall.

## Off-Season Changes

**Captaincy:** Riyan Parag (14.00 Cr, retained) continues as captain, having assumed the role in 2025 after Sanju Samson was traded to CSK. At 24, Parag is the youngest captain in the IPL 2026 cycle. His on-field maturity will be tested by the task of integrating six new faces into a squad that lost 9 of its last 10 matches.

**Key Departures:** Sanju Samson (to CSK, traded), Yuzvendra Chahal (to KKR, traded in 2025), Jos Buttler (retired), Trent Boult (released in 2024), Wanindu de Silva (not retained), Maheesh Theekshana (not retained). The cumulative impact is massive: Samson contributed 893 runs at 147.7 SR across the 2023-2024 window; Chahal took 39 wickets in those same two seasons. Buttler's 392 runs in 2023 at 131.5 SR had already declined. But Samson's departure removes the franchise's all-time leading scorer and its emotional anchor. This is a squad that must forge a new identity.

**Key Retentions:** Jaiswal (18.00 Cr), Parag (14.00 Cr), Jurel (14.00 Cr), Archer (12.50 Cr), Hetmyer (11.00 Cr), Deshpande (6.50 Cr), Sandeep Sharma (4.00 Cr). The retention strategy signals a commitment to youth and pace. Four of the seven retained players are 25 or younger. Sandeep Sharma at 4.00 Cr is the value retention: 7.38 powerplay economy across 52 overs (HIGH) and 10.07 death economy across 45.5 overs (HIGH).

**Auction Strategy:** Total squad salary of 125.40 Cr. The marquee acquisitions were all trades and auction: Jadeja (17.50 Cr, traded), Curran (10.00 Cr, traded), Bishnoi (7.20 Cr, auction). The remaining 10 auction buys cost a combined 3.80 Cr, all at base price or near it. This is a franchise that concentrated its spending on proven performers and filled the bench with budget options.

**What's Structurally Different from 2025:** Three things. First, spin bowling is no longer a weakness. Jadeja (7.79 middle econ, HIGH) and Bishnoi (8.57 middle econ, HIGH) replace de Silva and Theekshana, who combined for 9.05 economy. Second, the death-overs batting gains genuine depth: Curran's 183.9 death SR (248 balls, MEDIUM) supplements Hetmyer (174.7 SR, 534 balls, HIGH) and Jurel (179.0 SR, 229 balls, MEDIUM). Third, the allrounder balance shifts dramatically. RR move from a specialist-heavy squad to one with three genuine allrounders (Jadeja, Curran, Parag) who can each contribute 2-4 overs. The captain now has bowling flexibility that the 2025 squad simply did not offer.

## New Additions

### Ravindra Jadeja (17.50 Cr, traded from CSK)

The most consequential acquisition of RR's off-season. Jadeja's middle-overs bowling economy of 7.79 across 135.2 overs (HIGH) is 0.97 runs per over better than the IPL league average of 8.76. He bowls 95.3% of his overs in the middle phase, making him a phase specialist of the highest order. His batting (140.1 SR, 541 balls, HIGH) adds lower-order stability, particularly at the death where his 152.1 SR across 1,195 all-time career balls (HIGH) provides a reliable 20-30 runs. At 37, the concern is workload and fielding range. The opportunity is that Jadeja fills two gaps simultaneously: RR's middle-overs bowling control and their left-arm spin depth.

### Sam Curran (10.00 Cr, traded from PBKS)

Curran arrives as a left-arm pace allrounder with death-overs credentials on both sides of the ball. His 183.9 SR at the death with the bat (248 balls, MEDIUM) is 13.7 points above the league average of 170.2. His bowling economy of 10.45 across 100.8 overs (HIGH) is expensive by headline numbers, but his death-overs wicket rate of 14 wickets in 29.8 overs (HIGH) tells the real story: he buys wickets at the death, even if the economy is not pretty. The fit with RR is logical. Curran provides left-arm angle in a pace attack that was exclusively right-arm in 2025. The concern: his powerplay bowling economy of 8.78 (41 overs, HIGH) is adequate, not elite, and his middle-overs economy of 11.00 (30 overs, HIGH) suggests he should be used sparingly between overs 7 and 15.

### Ravi Bishnoi (7.20 Cr, auction)

Bishnoi returns to the IPL auction circuit after two seasons with Lucknow Super Giants. His middle-overs economy of 8.57 across 106 overs (HIGH) is solid, and his 28 wickets in that phase give him genuine wicket-taking capability. Against Delhi Capitals, he has 7 wickets at 9.00 economy in 114 balls (HIGH). The fit alongside Jadeja is the key: Bishnoi bowls leg-spin, turning the ball away from left-handers, while Jadeja's left-arm orthodox turns it in. Opposition batting lineups will face two distinct spin threats through the middle overs. The flag: Bishnoi's COMFORTABLE-band economy of 11.70 (70 balls, MEDIUM) suggests he can leak runs when pressure is low.

### Donovan Ferreira (0.75 Cr, auction)

South African wicketkeeper-batter with minimal IPL data (14 balls, 64.3 SR, LOW). His cross-tournament profile is more encouraging: 172 weighted SR across 1,127 T20 balls (trust score 46) in SA20, CSA, and the Blast. Projects as a backup keeper and potential middle-order option. At 0.75 Cr, the risk is minimal.

**Auction Grade: B+.** The trades of Jadeja and Curran directly address the two structural weaknesses (middle-overs bowling, death-overs flexibility) that sank the 2025 campaign. Bishnoi adds spin depth at fair value. The bench, however, is thin on proven IPL talent. If injuries strike the starting XI, the fallback options are limited.

## Team Style Analysis

RR's tactical identity has undergone the most dramatic transformation in the league over the past three seasons. From Buttler-Samson conservatism to Jaiswal-led aggression, the numbers tell the story in sharp detail.

**2023: The Chahal Era**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 148.2 | 7.68 |
| Middle | 129.7 | 8.77 |
| Death | 178.6 | 10.04 |

In 2023, RR's identity was powerplay batting (Jaiswal 176.1 PP SR) coupled with elite powerplay and death bowling. Chahal's 21 wickets at 7.99 economy and Boult's 13 at 8.07 gave the attack genuine teeth. The death bowling economy of 10.04 was among the best in the league. But the middle-overs batting SR of 129.7, well below the league average of 140.3, revealed a squad that consolidated rather than accelerated through the middle phase.

**2024: The Playoff Push**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy | Delta from 2023 |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 137.0 | 8.47 | Bat: -11.2 / Bowl: +0.79 |
| Middle | 136.5 | 8.43 | Bat: +6.8 / Bowl: -0.34 |
| Death | 173.0 | 10.49 | Bat: -5.6 / Bowl: +0.45 |

Powerplay batting declined significantly (-11.2 SR), but the middle overs improved on both sides of the ball: batting SR up 6.8, bowling economy down 0.34. This was the Parag effect. His 573 runs and 140.8 SR stabilized the middle order. The death bowling conceded 0.45 more, a warning sign that went unheeded.

**2025: The Collapse**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy | Delta from 2024 |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 174.4 | 9.31 | Bat: +37.4 / Bowl: +0.84 |
| Middle | 139.4 | 9.39 | Bat: +2.9 / Bowl: +0.96 |
| Death | 160.4 | 12.37 | Bat: -12.6 / Bowl: +1.88 |

The powerplay batting exploded to 174.4 SR, the highest in the league. The death bowling imploded to 12.37 economy, an increase of 1.88 RPO from 2024. This is a +1.88 death bowling regression in a single season. The middle-overs bowling worsened by 0.96. For context, a 0.96 RPO regression across 9 overs per match equates to approximately 8.6 additional runs conceded per match. Over 14 matches, that is roughly 120 runs given away that did not exist a year earlier.

**Squad-Building Philosophy:** The 2026 squad represents a deliberate pivot. Where the 2025 RR relied on powerplay batting to outscore bowling deficiencies, the 2026 version is built to control the middle overs through Jadeja and Bishnoi, exploit the powerplay through Jaiswal, and compete at the death through allrounder depth (Curran, Hetmyer, Jurel). The philosophy has shifted from "score more than we concede" to "concede less than everyone else in the middle, and trust the batting to do the rest."

The coaching staff must decide whether Jadeja bats through the middle or whether Parag and Jurel should be promoted ahead of him to inject scoring rate. If RR bat first, target 175+.
