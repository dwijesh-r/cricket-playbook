## The Headline

**The Perpetual Rebuild Finally Built Something. Now They Have to Prove It Wasn't an Accident.**

Punjab Kings finished 2025 with a 55.6% win rate and a trip to the final, their best campaign in over a decade. The squad that was supposed to be transitioning was suddenly contending. Shreyas Iyer (26.75 Cr, retained) scored 604 runs at 167.8 SR, Prabhsimran Singh matured into a genuine powerplay force at 159.3 SR, and Arshdeep Singh's economy improved to 8.62. They retained 20 of 22 players. The question is no longer whether Punjab can assemble talent. It is whether the 2025 breakthrough was the beginning of a cycle or a one-season anomaly inflated by a favourable draw and a death-overs explosion that may not repeat.

---

## The Story

The 2025 season was, by any measure, the most significant in Punjab Kings' recent history. After finishing 10th in 2023 (42.9%) and 9th in 2024 (35.7%), the franchise reversed course so dramatically that the trajectory resembles less a rebuild and more an awakening. Ten wins in 18 matches. A run to the IPL final. And a team that, for the first time since 2014, looked like it belonged at the business end of the tournament.

The transformation was powered by three engines. First, the batting exploded. Powerplay SR leapt from 142.1 in 2024 to 159.1 in 2025, a 17-point surge driven by Priyansh Arya's 175.8 SR debut campaign and Prabhsimran Singh's evolution from inconsistent hitter (146.1 SR in 2023) to composed aggressor (159.3 in 2025). Shreyas Iyer's 604 runs at 167.8 SR gave the middle order an anchor it had never had. Second, the death-overs batting hit 190.1 SR (up from 170.3 in 2024), with Shashank Singh delivering 201.6 SR in the final five overs. Third, Arshdeep Singh's economy improved from 9.46 to 8.62, providing the bowling attack with a genuine new-ball threat.

The off-season strategy was restrained. The franchise retained 20 players, adding only Cooper Connolly (1.00 Cr, auction) and Ben Dwarshuis (0.30 Cr, auction) as fresh faces. This is a squad that believes the core is right. The central question for 2026 is whether the 2025 improvements were structural or circumstantial. The death batting at 190.1 SR was elite, but it relied heavily on a small group: Shashank Singh (201.6 SR, 187 balls), Stoinis (178.2 SR, 481 balls — total career T20 sample, not death-specific), and Iyer (230.8 SR, 78 balls, LOW). The bowling, meanwhile, conceded 11.38 economy at the death in 2025, virtually unchanged from 11.39 in 2024. The middle-overs bowling went from 8.60 to 9.34, a regression that went largely unnoticed because the batting covered it.

If the batting sustains its 2025 levels and the bowling can tighten by even half a run per over in the middle overs, Punjab are a genuine top-four contender. If the batting regresses to the mean and the bowling stays where it is, this is a team that slides back to 7th or 8th. There is no middle ground. The 2026 squad is the 2025 squad with a year's more confidence and two more bodies. Everything rides on repetition.

## Off-Season Changes

**Captaincy:** Shreyas Iyer (26.75 Cr, retained) continues as captain after leading the franchise to its first final since 2014. His 604 runs in 2025 at 167.8 SR silenced doubts about whether a 26.75 Cr price tag could justify itself. It did. His leadership in 2026 will be measured by a different standard: can he replicate the performance when opponents have a full season of data on how the team functions?

**Key Departures:** Effectively none. Punjab retained 20 of their 22-player squad. The only notable absences are fringe players who were never in the predicted XII. This is the most continuity-heavy squad in the league.

**Key Retentions:** The headline retention is the spine: Iyer (26.75 Cr), Arshdeep (18.00 Cr), Chahal (18.00 Cr), Jansen (11.25 Cr), and Stoinis (11.00 Cr). Shashank Singh (5.50 Cr) represents outstanding value given his death-overs output. Prabhsimran Singh (4.00 Cr) is the cheapest genuine powerplay batter in the top four franchises.

**Auction Strategy:** Punjab spent 1.30 Cr at auction on two players: Cooper Connolly (1.00 Cr), a 22-year-old Australian left-arm orthodox all-rounder with 137.7 SR and 6.8 economy in BBL (HIGH trust, 45 confidence), and Ben Dwarshuis (0.30 Cr), a 31-year-old left-arm pace all-rounder with 8.2 economy across 3,303 T20 balls (HIGH). The message is clear: this team doesn't need new faces. It needs the same faces performing at the same level.

**Auction Grade: B+.** You cannot grade an auction that only spent 1.30 Cr on the same scale as teams that rebuilt from scratch. The real "auction" happened in the retention window, where Punjab kept their entire core intact. The grade reflects the retention strategy more than the two-player auction. Connolly adds left-arm orthodox depth (currently rated thin), and Dwarshuis provides another left-arm pace option. Neither is expected to start. This is a squad that bet on itself.

**What's Structurally Different from 2025:** Almost nothing, and that is the point. The XII is identical. The overseas combination (Stoinis, Omarzai, Jansen, Ferguson) is unchanged. The only structural shift is the addition of left-arm options (Connolly's orthodox spin, Dwarshuis's pace) that give the coaching staff slightly more variety in matchup-specific situations. For a franchise that changed its entire roster annually for the better part of a decade, continuity is itself the strategy.

## New Additions

### Cooper Connolly (1.00 Cr, Auction)

A 22-year-old Australian left-handed batting all-rounder who bowls left-arm orthodox. In the BBL, Connolly has struck at 137.7 SR across 624 balls (HIGH) with an average of 28.5, and his bowling economy of 6.8 in BBL (432 balls, HIGH) is genuinely impressive. He fills the left-arm orthodox spin gap that currently relies entirely on Harpreet Brar (87.2 overs, HIGH). Uncapped in IPL, his role is bench depth with a view to rotation. The concern: BBL conditions are materially different from Indian surfaces, and his SR of 103.7 in MLC (MEDIUM) suggests his batting may not translate universally.

### Ben Dwarshuis (0.30 Cr, Auction)

A 31-year-old Australian left-arm pace all-rounder with 234 T20 wickets at 8.2 economy across 3,303 balls (HIGH). At 0.30 Cr, this is pure insurance. He provides a left-arm pace angle that complements Arshdeep Singh and Marco Jansen, though his BBL economy of 8.0 (HIGH) is stronger than his PSL figure of 10.2 (MEDIUM). Uncapped in IPL. If Ferguson or Jansen is injured, Dwarshuis is the most experienced replacement available.

## Team Style Analysis

Punjab's tactical identity underwent a complete transformation across three seasons. What was once an inconsistent, mid-table squad with no clear philosophy has evolved into an attack-first batting unit that compensates for its bowling weaknesses through sheer scoring power.

**2023: The Directionless Season**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 136.1 | 9.23 |
| Middle | 129.0 | 8.96 |
| Death | 185.5 | 10.69 |

In 2023, Punjab's batting was below league average in both the powerplay and middle overs. Only the death batting (185.5 SR) showed genuine quality, but the bowling leaked across all three phases. The 8.96 middle-overs economy, while seemingly adequate, masked a team that conceded too many runs in the powerplay and never built pressure.

**2024: Still Searching**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy | Delta from 2023 |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 142.1 | 9.05 | Bat: +6.0 / Bowl: -0.18 |
| Middle | 137.0 | 8.60 | Bat: +8.0 / Bowl: -0.36 |
| Death | 170.3 | 11.39 | Bat: -15.2 / Bowl: +0.70 |

The middle overs improved in both batting and bowling, but the death batting collapsed by 15 points and the death bowling worsened to 11.39 economy. Five wins in 14 matches. The pieces were forming, but the death-overs execution was catastrophic.

**2025: The Breakthrough**

| Phase | Batting SR | Bowling Economy | Delta from 2024 |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Powerplay | 159.1 | 9.41 | Bat: +17.0 / Bowl: +0.36 |
| Middle | 147.6 | 9.34 | Bat: +10.6 / Bowl: +0.74 |
| Death | 190.1 | 11.38 | Bat: +19.8 / Bowl: -0.01 |

Everything clicked offensively. Powerplay SR surged to 159.1, middle overs hit 147.6, and the death batting exploded to 190.1. But the bowling tells a different story: economy worsened in the powerplay (9.41, up from 9.05) and the middle overs (9.34, up from 8.60). The death bowling stayed flat at 11.38. Punjab won through outscoring opponents, not through bowling discipline. That is both the identity and the fragility.

**Squad-Building Philosophy:** Punjab have built an all-rounder-heavy squad (9 of 22 players) that prioritizes batting depth over bowling specialization. With Stoinis, Shashank Singh, Omarzai, Jansen, and Harpreet Brar all offering dual utility, the XII can bat to #8 comfortably. The philosophy is unmistakable: outscore the opposition. The 2025 evidence suggests it works. The question is whether it works for two consecutive years.

### Chase Results by Target Band

### What This Means for 2026

The data says Punjab should chase when given the option. Their 54.5% chase win rate versus 37.5% when setting is a 17-point gap that reflects structural strengths: middle-overs batters who accelerate with a target, a death-overs unit (Shashank, Stoinis) that scales to required rates, and a bowling attack that performs better defending than bowling first. The coaching staff should elect to field first at neutral venues. At Mullanpur, where the average score is 165.4, chasing 170 is a realistic ask for this batting lineup.

The concern is what happens when Punjab are asked to set. Their setting win rate of 37.5% suggests a team that either doesn't post enough or can't defend what it posts. Given the death bowling economy of 11.16 across three seasons, the second explanation is more likely. When Punjab bat first, they need to post 190+ to give their bowling attack a defendable margin. That requires the top order to fire and the death batting to hit its 190.1 SR ceiling.

## The Bold Take

**Yuzvendra Chahal, not Arshdeep Singh, is the most irreplaceable bowler in Punjab's squad.**

Every fan and analyst would name Arshdeep as Punjab's bowling cornerstone, and the headline numbers support it: 57 wickets, 9.64 economy, 159.5 overs (HIGH). But consider the replacement scenario. If Arshdeep is injured, Punjab have Jansen (10.12 econ, 84.5 overs), Omarzai (9.92 econ, 48 overs), Vyshak (10.40 econ, 55.5 overs), and Yash Thakur (10.59 econ, 73.8 overs) as pace alternatives. The economy worsens by 0.5-1.0 runs, but the function (powerplay pace + death-overs execution) can be approximated.

Now remove Chahal. His death-overs bowling (9.88 economy, 33 overs, HIGH, 21 wickets) is genuinely unique. No other spinner in the squad bowls at the death with any reliability. Harpreet Brar's death economy is 10.53 (8.2 overs), and that sample is barely functional. Without Chahal, the captain loses the ability to bowl a spinner in overs 17-20, which means four pace overs at the death against a league where batters hit at 170.2 SR at the death. His middle-overs economy of 8.65 (119.8 overs, HIGH) is also critical: Brar at 7.21 (63 overs) is excellent but bowls far fewer overs. The tactical flexibility that Chahal provides (bowling 3 middle overs and 1 death over, or 2 and 2) is the foundation of Punjab's bowling plan. Without it, the attack becomes one-dimensional pace, and one-dimensional pace at Mullanpur, with its death-overs RPO of 10.44, is a guaranteed 200+ concession.

At 18.00 Cr, Chahal is expensive. He is also irreplaceable.

## Keys to Victory

**1. The Death Bowling Must Improve by at Least 0.5 RPO**

Punjab's death bowling economy of 11.16 across three seasons (1,143 balls) is the team's defining weakness. At 3.5/10, it is the lowest phase rating in the entire squad profile. Arshdeep (9.96 death econ) is the only sub-10 option. The coaching staff must commit to a death-overs plan: Arshdeep bowls overs 18 and 20, Chahal gets over 17 or 19, and one of Jansen/Ferguson takes the remaining over. If the combined death economy drops from 11.16 to 10.50 or below, Punjab's bowling moves from liability to functional. Every half-run improvement in the death translates to approximately 2.5 fewer runs conceded per match, which over 14 matches is the difference between 4th and 7th.

**2. Win at Home**

A 26.7% home win rate is not sustainable for a team with playoff ambitions. Punjab must win at least 4 of their 7 home matches (estimated) in 2026 to be in contention. The tactical adjustment is clear: bowl first at Mullanpur, target 165-170, and chase with the batting lineup. If the franchise insists on batting first at home, the death bowling economy of 11.16 will continue to undermine even strong first-innings totals.

**3. Manage the Overseas Quartet Without Rotation**

Punjab's predicted XII includes four overseas players: Stoinis, Omarzai, Jansen, and Ferguson. Unlike CSK, which has 8 overseas options competing for 4 slots, Punjab's overseas selection is essentially fixed. If any of the four are injured, the replacements are Bartlett (0.80 Cr, 10 overs of IPL data), Connolly (uncapped IPL), or Dwarshuis (uncapped IPL). The quality drop-off is severe. The coaching staff must manage workloads proactively, particularly for Jansen (who bowls powerplay and death) and Ferguson (34 years old, express pace). An injury to Stoinis, who provides both middle-order batting and part-time pace, would be the most damaging: no bench player replicates his dual function.

**4. Solve the Left-Arm Orthodox Vulnerability Before Opponents Exploit It**

The squad-wide SR of approximately 111 against left-arm orthodox (Prabhsimran 111.1, 81 balls, LOW; Shashank 100.0, 51 balls, LOW) is the most targetable matchup in the entire batting profile. Teams with Axar Patel, Ravindra Jadeja, or Mitchell Santner will bowl 6-8 overs of left-arm orthodox in the middle overs specifically to exploit this. The coaching staff needs a tactical response: either promote Wadhera (124.2 SR vs LA Orthodox, 33 balls, LOW, but better than the alternatives) or use Iyer (134.3 SR vs LA Orthodox, 67 balls, LOW) to face the majority of left-arm spin. This is not a problem that solves itself. It requires a deliberate batting-order adjustment for specific matchups.
